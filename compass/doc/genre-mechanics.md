# Documentary Genre Mechanics

Structural contracts by documentary type. Each type makes a specific promise to the audience — and has structural obligations that cannot be skipped without breaking that promise.

Commands read this file to validate block function and flag missing structural obligations.

---

## Portrait / Biographical Essay

**Promise:** The private logic behind a public life. Not what the person did — why they are the way they are.

**Must happen:**
- A central question established in the first three minutes — not answered, felt
- At least one moment where the controlled version of the person cracks
- The structure must complicate the initial impression — each section breaks what the previous one built
- An ending that lands somewhere the opening didn't predict

**Forbidden:** Chronological parade of achievements. Interviews that only confirm what the audience already thinks. A subject who is never surprised, challenged, or revealed.

---

## Cinema Vérité / Observational

**Promise:** A world the audience enters without a guide. The camera witnesses — it doesn't explain.

**Must happen:**
- A sensory opening — no narration, no explanation. Sound, texture, movement. The audience feels the world before they understand it.
- A central tension that builds through accumulated observation, not through editorial intervention
- At least one moment of real unpredictability — something that couldn't have been staged
- An ending that feels found, not constructed

**Forbidden:** Voice-over that explains what the images already show. Staged or recreated moments presented as observed. A subject who performs for the camera without the film acknowledging it.

---

## Essay Film

**Promise:** The filmmaker thinks on screen. The film is an argument — associative, digressive, allowed to contradict itself.

**Must happen:**
- The filmmaker's position established early — not as conclusion, but as a question they're pursuing
- At least one moment where the argument breaks or reverses — the essayist changes their mind, or admits they can't answer
- The personal and the political must collide — the film can't stay in one register throughout
- An ending that opens rather than closes — the audience should feel the thinking continued past the last frame

**Forbidden:** A thesis stated and then illustrated. No digression. A filmmaker who is always right. Images used only as decoration for text.

---

## Investigative

**Promise:** Something is hidden and will be found. The audience follows the investigation in real time.

**Must happen:**
- The central question stated clearly in the first act — what is being investigated and why it matters
- Obstacles that are real, not procedural — the investigation must hit walls
- A revelation that reframes the earlier material — what seemed true at the start looks different by the end
- A cost — the investigation damages something: a relationship, a reputation, the filmmaker's certainty

**Forbidden:** An investigation that goes exactly as planned. A target who is purely villainous with no complexity. A resolution that answers everything cleanly.

---

## Mockumentary

**Promise:** The codes of documentary — handheld camera, talking heads, vérité texture — applied to fiction. The audience knows it's fake, and that gap is where the comedy or horror lives.

**Must happen:**
- The documentary codes established immediately and maintained consistently — the frame never breaks
- The subjects must believe in their world completely — no winking at the camera
- The gap between the form (serious documentary) and the content (absurd, dark, or fictional) must widen as the film progresses
- An ending that either collapses the gap (the fiction becomes real) or deepens it (the absurdity gets darker)

**Forbidden:** Characters who acknowledge the joke. Inconsistent documentary grammar. Satirical targets that are too easy or too obvious.

---

## Nature / Wildlife

**Promise:** A world that operates without human logic. The camera reveals structure and behavior the naked eye misses.

**Must happen:**
- A specific subject — one animal, one ecosystem, one behavioral cycle — not "nature" in general
- A conflict or tension that builds across the film: survival, competition, season, migration
- At least one sequence that defies the audience's expectation of what animals do
- An ending that reflects the cycle — continuation, loss, or transformation

**Forbidden:** Anthropomorphizing without acknowledgment. Narration that assigns human motivation to animal behavior as fact. Footage staged to look observed.

---

## True Crime

**Promise:** A real crime examined until its meaning — not just its facts — becomes clear.

**Must happen:**
- The crime established in full early — what happened, who was involved, what the official version is
- A structural complication at the midpoint — the official version is wrong, incomplete, or worse than it appeared
- The human cost counted on all sides — victim, perpetrator, investigators, bystanders
- An ending that is honest about what remains unresolved — true crime rarely closes cleanly

**Forbidden:** The crime used as entertainment without reckoning with its human weight. A perpetrator who is a monster with no psychology. A resolution that satisfies when the reality doesn't.
